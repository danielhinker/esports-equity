The Offering Process

The offer is created whenever the company first contacts us through our website. From there, any investor looking at the offering will first make an account, receive the educational materials required, and then navigate to the offering they are interested in. From there, they will look at the details regarding the offering and if they choose, they can invest. They will be redirected to Transact API’s page where they will conduct the transaction.

The securities will be kept and recorded with Transact API.

There are several risks associated with purchasing securities, even more so when talking about the types of companies that tend to be featured on websites such as this one. These are startup companies with high rates of failure so unless you can afford to lose your entire investment, then we highly suggest you choose not to.

On Securities

The securities we offer are equity and debt based meaning that you either receive equity in a company for your investment or you are offering a loan to the company which will eventually be payed back with interest.

The risks involved with equity securities include the loss of your entire investment and the potential loss of voting power as more investors are brought on later. While you didn’t lose any shares, the number of shares increases so your voting power lessens.

Resale Restrictions

Securities bought through Title 3 of the JOBS ACT are very illiquid. There is no secondary market for these securities meaning they cannot be listed on any exchange and cannot be transferred for one year from the purchase date except for specific instances which include

Being transferred to an accredited investor
Being bought back by the issuing company
Being transferred to immediate family
Being transferred in the case of the owner's death

But even after one year it is still possible that the securities themselves will be illiquid.

An issuer also must include a discussion of any material changes or trends known to management in the financial condition and results of the operations of the issuer subsequent to the period for which financial statements are provided.

Issuers also should discuss how the proceeds from the offering will affect the issuer's liquidity, whether receiving these funds and any other additional funds is necessary to the viability of the business, and how quickly the issuer anticipates using its available cash. Cancellations

Our policy for cancellation is that the sale of securities is final except for accidental purchases. There will be a three day refund starting from the moment they receive their confirmation email.
(1) If there is a material change to the terms of an offering or to the information provided by the issuer, the intermediary must give or send to any investor who has made an investment commitment notice of the material change and that the investor's investment commitment will be cancelled unless the investor reconfirms his or her investment commitment within five business days of receipt of the notice. If the investor fails to reconfirm his or her investment within those five business days, the intermediary within five business days thereafter must:
(i) Give or send the investor a notification disclosing that the commitment was cancelled, the reason for the cancellation and the refund amount that the investor is expected to receive; and
(ii) Direct the refund of investor funds.
(2) If material changes to the offering or to the information provided by the issuer regarding the offering occur within five business days of the maximum number of days that an offering is to remain open, the offering must be extended to allow for a period of five business days for the investor to reconfirm his or her investment.
Issuers will generally not have cancellation rights except for instances where they do not reach their minimum offering raise.
